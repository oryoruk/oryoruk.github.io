January 1977

The Cato Institute is founded by Edward H. Crane and Charles G. Koch in San Francisco.

February 1978

Cato launches Byline, its daily public affairs radio program. The program is broadcast in more than 260 cities, including most of the nation’s largest markets.

June 1978

Cato holds its first Summer Seminar in Political Economy. Among the speakers are Murray Rothbard, Roy Childs, Leonard Liggio, Israel Kirzner, and Walter Williams.

Loading

January 1979

Cato publishes the first issue of Policy Report (now Cato Policy Report).

May 1, 1979

Cato publishes two classic manuscripts by Nobel Laureate F. A. Hayek: A Tiger by the Tail: The Keynesian Legacy of Inflation and Unemployment and Monetary Policy: Government as Generator of the “Business Cycle.”

Loading

January 15, 1980

Cato publishes its first Policy Analysis on corporate welfare and the Chrysler bailout.

Loading

October 1980

Cato publishes Peter Ferrara’s 500-page Social Security: The Inherent Contradiction which develops the first case for privatization.

Loading

March 1981

Cato publishes the first issue of the Cato Journal. Among the contributors to that inaugural issue are Arthur Ekirch, D.N. McCloskey, and Arthur Laffer.

Loading

January 1982

Cato relocates to Washington, D.C., and moves into the Watterston House on Capitol Hill.

February 1982

Cato publishes Freedom, Feminism, and the State, with contributors including Wendy McElroy, Rose Wilder Lane, Sarah and Angelina Grimke, Lysander Spooner, and Jean Bethke Elshtain.

Loading

May 1982

Cato publishes Solidarnosc z Wolnoscia (Solidarity with Liberty), which contains essays by F. A. Hayek, Milton Friedman, Ludwig von Mises, Michael Polanyi, and others, and smuggles the book into Poland. The Polish embassy tells CNN that this project is “insane.”

Loading

December 1, 1982

Nobel Laureate F. A. Hayek, a Cato distinguished senior fellow, is Cato’s first Distinguished Lecturer.

Loading

January 21, 1983

Cato hosts its first annual monetary conference, “The Search for Stable Money.” Among the participants are James Buchanan, Fritz Machlup, Karl Brunner, Allan Meltzer, Anna Schwartz, Gottfried Haberler, and Leland Yeager.

1984

Cato publishes Friedman and Hayek on Freedom in Russian and manages to get copies into the Soviet Union.

Loading

October 1984

Cato hosts a conference titled “Economic Liberties and the Judiciary,” at which future Supreme Court Justice Antonin Scalia and University of Chicago Professor Richard Epstein spar over the issue of economic freedom.

Loading

May 1985

William A. Niskanen, formerly of President Reagan’s Council of Economic Advisers, becomes chairman of the Cato Institute’s Board of Directors.

Loading

September 1986

Newsweek says Cato “has helped change the terms of debate by challenging Washington’s conventional wisdom with a provocative appeal for the future.”

Loading

May 19, 1987

Cato celebrates its tenth anniversary. The New York Times writes, “Cato has managed to generate more activity and interest across a wider political spectrum than some of its more sedate competitors with much larger budgets.”

Loading

October 2, 1987

Ed Crane talks with Clarence Thomas at Cato’s conference, “Assessing the Reagan Years.”

Loading

April 1988

Cato’s day-long conference on the privatization of the postal service is picketed by postal union activists.

Loading

September 1, 1988

Cato holds its first conference in China, titled “Economic Reform in China: Problems and Prospects.” It is believed to be the first conference on free markets and the rule of law ever held in the world’s most populous country. Speakers include Nobel Laureate Milton Friedman, economist Steven Cheung, and author George Gilder.

Loading

1989

Cato establishes its Center for Constitutional Studies.

Loading

1989

Anti-apartheid crusader Helen Suzman after delivering a Distinguished Lecture on the prospects for change in South Africa.

Loading

January 1990

Cato acquires Regulation magazine from the American Enterprise Institute.

Loading

1990

Ed Crane takes a snapshot at Cato’s open forum in Moscow, which draws more than 800 participants. He also presents a bust of F. A. Hayek to Yevgeny Primakov, chairman of the Council of the Union of the Supreme Soviet. Speakers include Charles Murray, James M. Buchanan, and emerging Russian liberals.

Loading

June 1990

Cato publishes its 50th book, Liberating Schools, edited by David Boaz.

Loading

January 1991

More than 300 people attend Cato’s conference on the Persian Gulf War.

Loading

January 30, 1992

The first Fiscal Policy Report Card on America’s Governors, then authored by Stephen Moore, is published.

Loading

May 1, 1992

Think tank president Luis Pazos, later chairman of the Mexican Congress’s budget committee, and Milton Friedman are among the speakers at Cato’s Mexico City conference, “Liberty in the Americas,” which draws more than 500 people from across the hemisphere.

Loading

January 1, 1993

Cato publishes Kindly Inquisitors: The New Attacks on Free Thought by Jonathan Rauch, a ground-breaking defense of free speech.

Loading

January 1, 1993 — 1994

The Cato Institute’s efforts to offer alternatives to First Lady Hillary Clinton’s health care plan, including the distribution of 300,000 copies of the book Patient Power, draw fire from the Clinton White House.

Nadine Strossen, then president of the American Civil Liberties Union, speaks at the grand opening dinner for Cato’s new building at 1000 Massachusetts Ave., N.W. Milton Friedman also speaks at the gala dinner, attended by 1000 guests.
